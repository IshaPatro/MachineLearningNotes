# 🎯 Decision Trees – Interview Questions

---

## 🟢 Easy

> [!question]- Why does splitting never increase MSE?
> Because the algorithm chooses the split only if it reduces MSE; otherwise, it can choose not to split, so MSE is non-increasing.

> [!question]- If a node contains only one data point, what is the MSE of that node?
> 
> $$
> \text{MSE} = 0
> $$
> 
> Because prediction equals the actual value.

> [!question]- Why is the mean recomputed after every split?
> Because each region has a different distribution of target values, and the mean minimizes MSE within that region.

> [!question]- What happens if all $y_i$ in a node are equal?
> 
> $$
> (y_i - \hat{y})^2 = 0
> $$
> 
> So MSE becomes zero and no further splitting is needed.

---

## 🟡 Medium

> [!question]- Why is decision tree splitting considered greedy?
> Because it optimizes MSE at the current node without considering future splits.

> [!question]- What is the difference between local and global optimization in trees?
> Local optimization minimizes MSE at each node independently, while global optimization would consider the entire tree structure.

> [!question]- Why do we use weighted MSE after a split?
> 
> $$
> \text{MSE}_{split} = \frac{n_L}{n}\text{MSE}_L + \frac{n_R}{n}\text{MSE}_R
> $$
> 
> Because each node contributes proportionally based on its size.

> [!question]- What happens if we don’t weight MSE after a split?
> Smaller nodes would influence the result equally as larger nodes, leading to incorrect split decisions.

> [!question]- Why don’t we try all possible real values of $s$?
> Because splits only change between consecutive sorted values, so midpoints are sufficient.

## 🔴 Hard

> [!question]- What is the worst-case depth of a decision tree?
> 
> $$
> \text{depth} = n
> $$
> 
> When each split isolates one data point.

> [!question]- Why are deep trees prone to overfitting?
> Because they memorize training data instead of learning general patterns.

> [!question]- What is the training MSE of a fully grown tree?
> 
> $$
> \text{Training MSE} = 0
> $$
> 
> If each leaf has one data point.

> [!question]- Why is MSE equal to variance within a node?
> 
> $$
> \text{MSE} = \frac{1}{n}\sum (y_i - \bar{y})^2 = \text{Var}(y)
> $$
> 
> Because prediction is the mean.

> [!question]- What happens if features are not sorted before splitting?
> Valid split points cannot be identified correctly, leading to incorrect partitions.

> [!question]- Can two different splits give the same MSE?
> Yes, especially when the distribution of target values is symmetric across splits.
